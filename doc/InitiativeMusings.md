# Initiative Principles

It is really easy to get bogged down in the minutiae of initiative.

To protect against that, it's useful to keep in mind the very simple 
purposes of initiative, and how they evolve in complexity.

## No initiative

AD&D could be played by ignoring initiative entirely:

1) The DM secretly notes intentions for the monsters
2) The players announce their intentions
3) Then go. Don't even roll initiative dice.

The end result would mean that every creature, player and monster alike, 
gets to finish their action. If they die, they get to complete their 
action first. It is completely impossible to prevent a spell caster
from completing their spell. Casting time and timed durations are 
irrelevant.

It's worth trying out sometime, to see how it feels. It's actually 
interesting. It encourages slightly different tactics, and spell casters
are very powerful, but it's still incremental - a "round" ends, 
creatures are affected or dead, and everyone can register intentions
once again that are appropriate for the changed situation.

## Simple initiative

The simplest form of initiative that is consistent with how it is 
described in the book - where high roll wins - is for each side to 
roll one die, and for the side with the high die to *complete* all their 
actions before the other side gets to *start* theirs. (The exception 
being actions that may not be completable within one round, like 
charging.)

The advantage over "No initiative" is just that creatures have a 
chance to affect or kill their opponents before it is the opponents'
turn. This is of course a key part of AD&D combat, that has major
impact in how closely-matched combats play out. It also makes it 
more possible to prevent dangerous opponents from ever even making
their first move - for instance, gaining initiative against a 
spellcaster and hitting them will cause them to lose their spell,
interrupting them from casting it.

So the basic concept of simple initiative is very valuable to 
players, but there's still one aspect that is completely uncovered:
timed actions, or actions with defined duration.

## AD&D Initiative

This is really the single concept that drove all the attempts at 
new initiative rules in AD&D: What if timed actions were introduced?

And this is where it really needs to be spelled out:

1) If there were no timed actions, every action would be instant.
2) If every action were instant, AD&D initiative would be identical
   to simple initiative.
3) Timed actions should *matter* somehow in AD&D combat
4) A timed action, by definition, takes **longer** than an instant action.
5) Therefore, timed actions should be **easier to interrupt** than instant
   actions.

So what does this mean? I believe the following implications follow
quite cleanly, when trying to interpret BtB rules:

1) An action that might normally win initiative could, in some 
   situations, still be interrupted if the action has duration
2) An action that would normally lose initiative should not
   become less likely to be interrupted if it has duration
3) An action with a longer duration should be more probable to 
   interrupt than an action with a shorter duration

### Misinterpretations

Recall that with simple initiative, gaining initiative on a caster
and then hitting the caster will cause them to lose their spell. 
No mention is made of spell length or when they start casting. 
If they are hit, they lose the spell.

With AD&D initiative, there is also a note in the DMG that combat
is designed to be biased against spell casters. Thus the note of 
how casters try to avoid combat, try to use wands and magic devices,
etc.

With both the above points in mind, it is hard to defend the 
interpretation that the introduction of durations should make it
*easier* for casters to complete spells.

There are no examples in the DMG of casters *losing* initiative, 
and being hit, but being able to cast their spell anyway due to 
casting time.

There are, however, two examples in the DMG of casters winning 
initiative, and still being susceptible to losing their spells 
due to casting time.

Imagine a spell with casting time 5 - a fairly lengthy spell.
Should that spell actually be easier to complete than a spell
in simple initiative? Or should it be harder, due to the 
additional risk of duration, even after winning initiative?

### Timed Actions and Durations

An action that requires time (and concentration) is fraught with
danger. It is *more* risky, not less, than an instant action. If
timed actions and durations are to matter somehow, that is clearly
the direction in which they are intended to matter.

## Rule Two

With that in mind, let's look at Rule #2 on page 65.

>"Attacks directed at spell casters will come on that segment of the 
> round shown on the opponent's or on their own side's initiative die, 
> whichever is applicable. (If the spell caster's side won the initiative 
> with a roll of 5, the attack must come then, not on the opponent's losing 
> roll of 4 or less.) Thus, all such attacks will occur on the 1st-6th 
> segments of the round." (DMG p65)

Leaving aside the confusion regarding the "whichever is applicable" 
phrase for the moment, let's examine the two clearer sentences and 
their implications.

First, we know that if a caster *wins* initiative, an attack directed
at them will come on the segment of the caster's die.

One implication: if the caster chooses to cast a spell with a six-
segment casting time, and they are attacked and hit, the attack will
*absolutely interrupt the spell*, even if the caster wins initiative.
