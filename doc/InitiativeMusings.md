# Initiative Principles

It is really easy to get bogged down in the minutiae of initiative.

To protect against that, it's useful to keep in mind the very simple purposes of initiative, and how they evolve in complexity.

## No initiative

AD&D could be played by ignoring initiative entirely:

1. The DM secretly notes intentions for the monsters
2. The players announce their intentions
3. Then go. Don't even roll initiative dice.

The end result would mean that every creature, player and monster alike, gets to finish their action. If they die, they get to complete their action first. It is completely impossible to prevent a spell caster from completing their spell. Casting time and timed durations are irrelevant.

It's worth trying out sometime, to see how it feels. It's actually interesting. It encourages slightly different tactics, and spell casters are very powerful, but it's still incremental - a "round" ends, creatures are affected or dead, and everyone can register intentions once again that are appropriate for the changed situation.

## Simple initiative

The simplest form of initiative that is consistent with how it is described in the book - where high roll wins - is for each side to roll one die, and for the side with the high die to _complete_ all their actions before the other side gets to _start_ theirs. (The exception being actions that may not be completable within one round, like charging.)

The advantage over "No initiative" is just that creatures have a chance to affect or kill their opponents before it is the opponents' turn. This is of course a key part of AD&D combat, that has major impact in how closely-matched combats play out. It also makes it more possible to prevent dangerous opponents from ever even making their first move - for instance, gaining initiative against a spellcaster and hitting them will cause them to lose their spell, interrupting them from casting it.

So the basic concept of simple initiative is very valuable to players, but there's still one aspect that is completely uncovered: timed actions, or actions with defined duration.

## AD&D Initiative

This is really the main concept that distinguishes AD&D initiative from simple initiative: What if timed actions were introduced?

And this is where it really needs to be spelled out:

1. If there were no timed actions, every action would be instant.
2. If every action were instant, AD&D initiative would be identical to simple initiative.
3. Timed actions should _matter_ somehow in AD&D combat
4. A timed action, by definition, takes **longer** than an instant action.
5. Therefore, timed actions should be **easier to interrupt** than instant actions.

So what does this mean? I believe the following implications follow quite cleanly, when trying to interpret BtB rules:

1. An action that might normally win initiative could, in some situations, still be interrupted if the action has duration
2. An action that would normally lose initiative should not become less likely to be interrupted if it has duration
3. An action with a longer duration should be more probable to interrupt than an action with a shorter duration

### Misinterpretations

Recall that with simple initiative, gaining initiative on a caster and then hitting the caster will cause them to lose their spell. No mention is made of spell length or when they start casting. If they are hit, they lose the spell.

With AD&D initiative, there is also a note in the DMG that combat is designed to be biased against spell casters. Thus the note of how casters try to avoid combat, try to use wands and magic devices, etc.

With both the above points in mind, it is hard to defend the interpretation that the introduction of durations should make it _easier_ for casters to complete spells.

There are no examples in the DMG of casters _losing_ initiative, and being hit, but being able to cast their spell anyway due to casting time.

There are, however, two examples in the DMG of casters winning initiative, and still being susceptible to losing their spells due to casting time.

Imagine a spell with casting time 5 - a fairly lengthy spell. Should that spell actually be easier to complete than a spell in simple initiative? Or should it be harder, due to the additional risk of duration, even after winning initiative?

An action that requires time (and concentration) is fraught with danger. It is _more_ risky, not less, than an instant action. If timed actions and durations are to matter somehow, that is clearly the direction in which they are intended to matter.

## Rule Two

With that in mind, let's look at Rule #2 on page 65.

> "Attacks directed at spell casters will come on that segment of the round shown on the opponent's or on their own side's initiative die, whichever is applicable. (If the spell caster's side won the initiative with a roll of 5, the attack must come then, not on the opponent's losing roll of 4 or less.) Thus, all such attacks will occur on the 1st-6th segments of the round." (DMG p65)

Leaving aside the confusion regarding the "whichever is applicable" phrase for the moment, let's examine the two clearer sentences and the implication.

First, we know that if a caster _wins_ initiative, an attack directed at them will come on the segment of the winning caster's die.

The implication: if the caster chooses to cast a spell with a seven-segment casting time, and they are attacked and hit, the attack will _absolutely interrupt the spell_, even if the caster wins initiative.

But if the caster loses initiative? The DMG does not state. There is a popular interpretation that Rule 2 also holds for when the caster loses initiative, but it leads to perverse results: an attacker's winning roll of 6-5 can lead to the caster succeeding in casting their spell even if hit, when their spell would have been interrupted under the simple initiative rules.

## A Cohesive Interpretation of Initiative

Initiative in AD&D starts to fall in place when we make some observations and apply some simple principles:

Observations:

1. The AD&D rulebook does not -- and is not able to -- exhaustively describe an example algorithm for every possible interaction of combat actions.
2. The various initiative rules laid out are laid in in a structure that follows the A-H listing at the beginning of the combat section.

Principles:

1. For a given combat situation, if no rule is explicitly stated, simple initiative is the fallback.
2. More specific rules are applied according to the circumstance and where it is placed in the A-H sequence.

A very clear example: Melee attacks against spell casters, which is covered in p66-67 of the DMG. It is true that there is an earlier section "Rule 2" on p65, that purports to cover attacks against spellcasters. But that is a more general rule, that is in the "Missile Weapons, Devices, Spells, and Turning" section. So the proper way to read p66-67 is that it is specific to melee weapons against timed-duration attacks.
